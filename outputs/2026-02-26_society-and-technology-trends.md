And this separation is Nash stable, which is a technical way of saying that once it happens, no individual agent has an incentive to change their behavior. The system becomes locked in place.

Let's unpack what this means. In the model, agents in the upper class play what's called "pure strategies." This means they consistently make the same choices, over and over. They've found a winning formula and they stick with it. They receive higher payoffs than everyone else. Crucially, all agents in the upper class receive identical rewards. They're equally successful.

Meanwhile, agents in the lower class all play the same mixed strategy, which means they're randomizing their choices. They're trying different approaches, but they're not succeeding as well. All agents in the lower class receive identical rewards to each other, but those rewards are lower than what the upper class receives.

This is remarkable because it shows that even in a system with no formal hierarchy, no government imposing class structure, no deliberate discrimination, classes naturally emerge. The system self-organizes into a stratified structure.

But it gets more interesting. As Gros increased the level of envy in his model—meaning, as agents became more sensitive to comparisons with others—the structure became more pronounced. The gap between classes grew. And the fraction of agents in the upper class decreased. More and more agents fell into the lower class. Eventually, with very high levels of envy, you end up with a single agent in the upper class and everyone else in the lower class.

Classes form. And as envy increases, inequality increases.
